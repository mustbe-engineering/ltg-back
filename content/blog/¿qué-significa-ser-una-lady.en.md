Geek spaces have historically been associated with men, and when we think of women within these worlds, the media has long sold us a rather limited image: the girl with a headset and pink cat ears, the e-girl aesthetic perfectly curated for social media. In comics, superheroines appear in outfits that, realistically, would be a wardrobe malfunction waiting to happen. And while there's nothing wrong with showing skin—because every woman can dress however she wants—the problem is that this has been practically the only available narrative.

But reality is different.

Magic has carried the image of the male player for years, the guy in the shop with his tournament list memorized. And it's not that this image is false, but it is incomplete. Because in Mexico, the community of women who play has been growing, and with it something interesting: we are not all the same, not even close.

Girls with gothic aesthetics, others who show up in jeans and sneakers, some with makeup that frankly is a work of art. Tall, short, with short hair, long hair, a thousand different colors. From different professions, ages, and tastes that probably have nothing in common outside the table.

And that's exactly what makes this worthwhile.

Seeing that diversity in a space that for so long didn't account for us says a lot. Every woman who sits down to play her deck is proof that this was never about just one type of person. And the community, when it truly opens itself up, is better for everyone.

There's no better way to understand what it means to be a Lady than to hear it from those who live it:

Lady Sofía describes it as something that goes far beyond the game: for her, being part of Ladies the Gathering has meant community, daring to try new things, and meeting incredible women with whom she has played, chatted, and learned. "I learn from the game and I learn from my teammates," she says, "with respect, fun, and sometimes with seriousness." For Sofía, this space has shown her the support and love that can exist between friends who want to have fun without being judged or objectified.

For Lady Erika, being part of Ladies the Gathering is a source of pride. She deeply values Vanessa's effort and the council's commitment to building a safe space where girls can learn, play, and spend time together. "Little by little, a beautiful friendship is forming," she shares, and she recognizes in this project something greater: a movement that has put women on the MTG map, empowering us and encouraging us to participate in increasingly competitive ways.

Lady Igreyne, on the other hand, says it with a clarity that needs no embellishment: "Being a lady is arriving sophisticated, with presence, to compete and master complex strategies." For her, being a lady is proving that you can do whatever you want at that table, stepping firmly where only others stepped before. Strength, unity, fun, and family: that's how she sums up what this space means.

And I, Lady Cinthya, add my voice to those words with a single conclusion:

Being a lady has nothing to do with how you look or how much you know about Magic. It's about showing up, sitting at the table, and playing. It's about finding a space where nobody questions whether you really know what you're doing, because you're already doing it. We are a community quite different from one another, but that is exactly what makes us Ladies the Gathering.
